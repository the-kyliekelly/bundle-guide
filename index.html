01
Section 01
You don't need to host anything to start.
Contributing to someone else's bundle is the lower-lift path and still gets serious results.
Average result: ~150 new subscribers per bundle. Some more, some less but Kylie's consistent result over her last six bundles was around 150. That's because she's strategic: aligned bundles, ideal client fit, a quality resource every time.
Is this attracting your ideal client? Non-negotiable. There is zero point in being in a bundle if your people won't be there. Every subscriber you add costs money on your email service provider they need to be the right ones.
10–30 contributors is the sweet spot. Over 50? Don't bother. Your resource gets completely lost, subscribers get overwhelmed, and you'll end up with low-quality leads bombarded by dozens of welcome sequences.
Vet the host before you say yes. Sign up to their email list if you're on the fence. See if they actually send emails, what the experience is like, what their vibe is. You're lending them the trust you've built with your audience make sure it's worth it.
Ask questions. It's allowed. Don't be afraid to ask who else is in it, what the contributor expectations are, how they're promoting it. A good host will answer without hesitation.
Never pay to contribute. Unless you're a sponsor getting extra visibility, you should not pay to be in a bundle. If money changes hands, the dynamic changes entirely. Not in your favour.
Build relationships with other contributors. 99% of people don't do this. But when you're in a bundle, you're surrounded by people who serve your same ideal client. Reach out. Have a coffee chat. This is where collaborations start.
Personalise your promo email. You'll get swipe copy from the host. Use it as a starting point, not a copy-paste. Add a story, tell them why you're excited, mention a gift you're grabbing yourself. Your audience can tell the difference.
💡The Takeaway
Being selective is the strategy. One aligned bundle beats five random ones every single time.
02
Section 02
Start with the end. Work backwards.
The right resource isn't random it's reverse-engineered from where you want them to end up.
Reverse-engineer from your main offer. What offer do you ultimately want them in: membership, course, 1:1, mastermind? Work backwards. Your bundle resource is the first step in that journey. Make it a no-brainer entry point.
If you don't have a main offer yet, that's okay. Think about what quick win would genuinely help your ideal client right now. Get them on your list, start building the relationship, and ask them what they need. Let the market tell you what to create.
Give them one specific win. Not a generic overview of everything you know. Something so good they think "I can't believe I got that for free." That's the reaction you're going for.
The format doesn't matter. Checklist, PDF, private audio, quiz, mini-course, AI tool. Whatever format works best for the content. The win is what matters, not the container.
Name it around the transformation, not the format. "Email Checklist" is forgettable. "The 15-Minute List Builder" stops the scroll. In a bundle with 10–30 other resources, your name is your hook. Use ChatGPT to brainstorm names if you're stuck.
Rotate your products. Don't put the same resource in every bundle for six months running. It devalues the product and means loads of your existing subscribers already have it. Mix it up or create something fresh specifically for the bundle.
Optimise your opt-in page. Mock-up image. Specific headline. Tiny bit of copy. Name and email only. Make it easy to say yes. A weak opt-in page wastes all the traffic the bundle sends you.
Add a tripwire or upsell if you can. A small offer ($9–47) on the thank-you page turns your bundle traffic into revenue. It's not essential for your first bundle, but start thinking about it after you've done one or two.
💡The Takeaway
Your bundle resource is the on-ramp. If it doesn't lead somewhere, you're building a list with no destination.
03
Section 03
It's less work than you think.
Far less work than a summit. Still work, but when the upside is 500–2,000 new subscribers, it's worth it.
Contribute first. Host when you're ready. Being in a bundle as a contributor shows you how they run, what you'd do differently, and who the good hosts are in your space. One or two bundles as a contributor gives you everything you need.
Be specific. Outcome-based. Slightly polarising. Not "a business bundle" but "list growth for coaches." The specific theme makes it easier to find contributors, easier to market, and easier to attract the exact right people. Generic bundles don't perform.
Keep it to 10–30 contributors. Curated beats crowded. Vet for alignment and quality. Your reputation is on the line too. Contributors are lending you their audience's trust.
Start with warm outreach. Your existing peers, past collaborators, podcast guests, people in communities you're already in. You'd be surprised how fast a list of 10–30 people comes together when you start from your network.
Give contributors everything they need. Clear expectations, a promotional kit, a timeline. Everyone promoting is non-negotiable. Make it easy for them: provide a swipe email they can personalise and send.
Tech you actually need: A sign-up page (name + email), a gift delivery page with product links, and a welcome email. You can do all of this with your existing ESP. A landing page in Kit, a Google Drive for delivery, an automated email. That's it.
3–5 days is the sweet spot. Long enough to build momentum, short enough to stay urgent. Any longer and signups slow and excitement drops.
All signups go to your list. Not just the people who grab your product every single person who signs up to the bundle. That's why hosting outpaces contributing for list growth. You own the event; you get the list.
💡The Takeaway
Start small. 10 contributors, a simple opt-in page, a gift delivery link page. That is enough. Build from there.
04
Section 04
The bundle ends. The relationship begins.
This is where signups become subscribers, and subscribers become buyers.
If you contributed: Your Start Here email goes out automatically when they grab your product. Make sure it's set up before the bundle goes live. Then keep sending your regular emails. Don't ghost them once they're on the list.
If you hosted: Wait about a week after the bundle closes. Let the noise die down. Then send a welcome email as the host introduce yourself, remind them how they found you, and ask them a question to get them engaging.
Ask how they found the bundle. What was their favourite gift? What would make it better next time? This doubles as a feedback form and a relationship-builder. People love being asked.
Add an engagement sequence if you only send weekly. If you email once a week normally, set up 2–3 extra emails for new bundle subscribers your best content, your values, a quick win. Fast-track the relationship instead of just dropping them into the regular schedule.
Make an offer. By the third email, invite them to something. They've been through an amazing bundle experience, they're warm. Pitch them. Use your four email types: connection, teaching, connection, invitation.
Track your results. Bundle name, resource you submitted, dates, number of leads. Go back and fill in the numbers after it closes. That data tells you whether to say yes next time, or pass.
Reach out to the host (and other contributors). If the bundle went well and audiences were aligned, that host is now a warm contact. Suggest a freebie swap, a podcast collab, a future bundle together. The relationship is where the real long-term value is.
💡The Takeaway
The bundle fills the list. The emails after are where the money actually gets made. Don't skip this part.
05
Section 05
Most bundles fail for the same reasons.
Not because of the strategy. Because of how it was implemented.
Saying yes to misaligned bundles. FOMO is real, but a bundle full of wrong-fit subscribers costs you more than it gains. You pay for every subscriber on your list. Wrong people = wasted money and terrible email metrics.
A weak opt-in page. No mock-up. Vague headline. Too many fields. If your opt-in page doesn't clearly communicate the win in five seconds, people scroll past. The bundle sends the traffic your page converts it.
A too-generic resource. "5 email tips" doesn't stop anyone. Your resource is often someone's first impression of your business. Make them think "I would have paid for this." Specific, actionable, transformation-focused.
No nurture plan. Getting the subscriber is step one. If you don't email until you're selling, you're leaving money on the table and training them to ignore you. Have a Start Here email and a plan for what comes after before the bundle goes live.
Not tracking results. If you can't remember which bundles worked and which didn't, you'll keep repeating the same experiments. Name, resource, dates, leads. Four columns in a spreadsheet is all it takes.
The same product in every bundle. Once or twice is fine. But if the same product has been in every bundle for six months, it loses perceived value, and a lot of your subscribers will already have it.
Going straight to hosting without contributing first. Contributing teaches you what good looks like, what you'd do differently, and who you want to work with again. Don't skip this step.
💡The Takeaway
Intentional beats prolific. One well-chosen bundle beats five random ones. Always.
